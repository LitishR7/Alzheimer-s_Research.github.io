Litish Rajesh Deepa  ·  Independent Research G/T

📄Research Abstract
Alzheimer's disease is a progressive neurodegenerative disorder characterized by cognitive decline, disrupted neural communication, and the accumulation of toxic proteins such as amyloid-beta and tau tangles. Current pharmacological approaches treat symptoms but do not reverse the underlying pathologies. Therefore, non-invasive neural entrainment has gained interest for its ability to target these root causes directly.
This study reviewed and synthesized recent clinical, preclinical, and meta-analytic research on 40-Hz gamma frequency stimulation (GENUS). Evidence suggests 40-Hz stimulation results in a 40% increase in interneuron firing consistency, an 11–14% increase in cognitive task accuracy, and decreases in Alzheimer's biomarkers including plasma pTau217 by 10–27% and beta-amyloid plaque deposition by 30–53%. Other evidence showed greater hippocampal synchrony, increased microglial activation, and enhanced glymphatic clearance during stimulation, particularly when synchronized with circadian rhythms. GENUS represents an intriguing intervention to be used alongside existing pharmacological and therapeutic strategies.

📝Literature Review & Methodology
For more than a century, the medical community accepted the "Amyloid Cascade Hypothesis," which argued that toxic proteins and plaques caused Alzheimer's pathological symptoms. However, due to repeated failures in drug trials where plaques were cleared but patient memory did not improve, the definition of neurodegeneration has been revisited. Currently, Alzheimer's disease is regarded as a failure of the brain's communication system at the neurological level.
The biomechanical process for achieving neural entrainment through GENUS is a synthesized interaction between physics and neurobiology. It operates on the foundational principle that the brain's internal electrical rhythms can be synchronized. In the case of Alzheimer's disease, this involves the delivery of synchronized 40Hz flashing lights and auditory tones, which force the primary visual and auditory cortices to fire at the exact same frequency they are being exposed to.

Research Journey

The process behind "A New Frequency"

From initial curiosity to published conclusions — 2025–2026

🗺How This Research Came Together
Behind every research paper is a process of false starts, surprising discoveries, and gradual clarity. This page documents the real journey behind this study — from the first spark of interest in Alzheimer's non-pharmacological treatments to the synthesis of data across dozens of peer-reviewed sources.
Early Fall 2025
The Initial Question
After learning about the scale of Alzheimer's disease and the consistent failures of amyloid-targeting drugs in clinical trials, the central question emerged: if clearing plaques doesn't restore memory, what actually drives the cognitive collapse? This led to the discovery of gamma oscillation research and the GENUS program at MIT.
October 2025
Literature Deep Dive
Spent several weeks building a structured literature base from PubMed Central, ClinicalTrials.gov, and Frontiers in Neuroscience. Key foundational papers by Buzsáki & Wang (2012) on gamma oscillation mechanisms and Jessen et al. (2015) on the glymphatic system became the theoretical backbone of the research design.
November 2025
Expert Interview — Dr. Raymond Turner
Conducted a primary research interview with Dr. Raymond Turner, whose expertise in neurodegenerative disease and clinical trial methodology provided critical real-world context for the preclinical-to-human translation gap — one of the study's most important limitation findings.
December 2025
Mid-Year Presentation
Presented initial findings to the G/T Advanced Research program, covering background on gamma waves, the core research question, early data, and proposed translational device concepts including gamma light-bulbs and audiovisual 40Hz stimulants. Received feedback that strengthened the methodology section.
January – February 2026
Data Synthesis & Analysis
Compiled and cross-referenced data from all sources into a unified analytical dataset. Constructed five figures examining physiological improvements, biomarker reductions, GENUS vs. pharmaceutical comparisons, power spectral density changes, and glymphatic clearance rates. The sex-based difference in pTau217 reduction (47% female vs. 19.7% male) was one of the most striking and unexpected findings.
February 27, 2026
Final Paper Submitted
The completed meta-analytical paper, "A New Frequency: How light and sound can influence your brain in Alzheimer's," was submitted. The research concluded that GENUS, while not yet a standalone cure, represents a powerful complementary intervention that addresses Alzheimer's pathology at its neurological roots rather than merely managing symptoms.
💡Key Takeaways from This Process
🔍
Question Everything
The most interesting finding came from questioning the dominant theory — the Amyloid Cascade Hypothesis — rather than accepting it at face value.
🤝
Primary Sources Matter
Interviewing Dr. Turner added a layer of real-world nuance that no peer-reviewed paper alone could have provided.
📊
Data Tells a Story
Visualizing the five data figures made patterns — like the sex-based biomarker disparity — immediately obvious in ways that text alone couldn't convey.
🚀
Science is Ongoing
The biggest limitation of this research is also its most exciting implication: GENUS human trials are just beginning, meaning the best findings are still ahead.
